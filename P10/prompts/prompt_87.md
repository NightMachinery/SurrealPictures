Opposite him, the Matchmaker—tender, unsettling, neither clearly young nor old, barefoot on mosaic shards, draped in layered fabrics that resemble wedding veils and mourning shrouds at once—leans in as if whispering a verdict; from their fingers spill delicate ribbons/threads that knot themselves between strangers in the crowd (hands almost touching, eyes averted), forming a living lattice of bonds and contradictions. Populate every corner: masked petitioners with empty frames instead of faces; lovers and rivals sharing a single shadow; a child-sized statue weeping sand; a giant wedding ring serving as an archway; broken clocks used as bowls; feathers, petals, and court documents swirling weightlessly; small animals perched in impossible places (a fox in a bishop’s chair, swallows nesting in a ribcage-shaped chandelier). Everything is paused at the brink—threads taut, sand mid-fall, cradle mid-sway—so the scene can read as longing, grief, desire, shame, tenderness, or dread depending on the viewer; no empty space, no readable text, no gore, no explicit sex, only vulnerable bodies and charged proximity rendered with classical restraint and emotional weight.
