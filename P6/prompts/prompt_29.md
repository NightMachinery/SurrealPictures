A crowded, labyrinthine clockwork orphanage that feels half-womb, half-machine-cathedral: every wall is a forest of puzzle doors (jigsaw seams, keyholes shaped like constellations, orphan nameplates etched into brass), stacked impossibly into a spiraling interior that curves like the inside of an astrolabe. In the center, a mythic dancer hangs in a frozen mid-leap—one bare foot en pointe on the edge of a gear the size of a moon, the other leg extended through a doorway that opens onto nothing but blinding starlight; her posture is both balletic and defiant, as if she is holding the entire building together with sheer poise. The literal metaphor of resilience is broken wide open: the orphanage is visibly collapsing inward, teeth of gears and doorframes shearing like ice—yet every crack is stitched shut by the dancer’s kite-string tendons, dozens of taut lines radiating from her wrists, ribs, and hairpins, laced through keyholes and pulleys, suspending shattered staircases and falling puzzle-doors in midair like a marionette show where the puppet masters the theater. A single flying kite—enormous, ceremonial, made from patched orphan uniforms and gilded paper—floats above her like a halo and a trap; its tail is a chain of tiny origami boats that sail through the air as if the room were an ocean, each boat carrying a sleeping clockwork baby, each baby holding a key that doesn’t fit any lock. Ribbon-wrapped gifts and unopened boxes crowd the foreground like offerings and burdens, their bows turning into mechanical knots; rose petals drift everywhere like slow red snow, snagging on gear-teeth and eyelashes, while nightingales (some living, some brass automatons) perch in doorframes and sing into trumpet-gramophones that exhale visible musical notation—paper strips that become new corridors. The scene is paused at the exact instant of impact: a swung pendulum the size of a bed hovers a hair’s breadth from the dancer’s throat; shattered glass from a stained window hangs as a perfect halo of shards; a spilled jar of honey is suspended in the air as golden globules that mimic miniature suns, lighting faces in the crowd of half-seen orphans behind doors (eyes watching, hands reaching, identities ambiguous).
