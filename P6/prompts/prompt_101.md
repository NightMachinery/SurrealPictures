A towering Inventor-priest stands on a narrow balcony of a walled old town whose streets are **mapped in luminous tile mosaics** like a living circuit diagram—turquoise, lapis, and gold tesserae climbing the walls, spilling into rooftops, pooling into alleys with no horizon anywhere—his posture half-bowed as if in confession, half-erect as if conducting an experiment; one bare shoulder and ribcage exposed like a classical statue, the rest wrapped in a regal coat of impossible cut, its surfaces **overlaid with flat, unmoving coral-and-pearl screen-tone patterns that do not follow folds**, sliding over him like a second, indifferent skin. In his gloved hands he holds a **stopped clock** split open into a reliquary, its hands pinned at an unreadable time; behind it, a mask of mirrored porcelain hovers inches from his face, and yet his face is also a mask—multiple profiles misaligned like misprinted posters—so the viewer cannot tell what is revealed and what is performed. The town itself participates in the illusion: mosaic doorways pretend to be windows, windows pretend to be saints’ niches, and every arch contains another smaller arch, a recursive theater of facades; wax seals stamped with unknown crests cling to bricks like barnacles, and signet rings hang from iron lanterns as if they were fruit. The defining impossible event is frozen mid-catastrophe: entire layers of the city **levitate**—a mosaic street lifts like a rug, rooftops float upward with their chimneys still smoking, a bell tower tilts in the air without falling—while the Inventor remains perfectly still, reverent, as if this weightless upheaval is a sacred rite rather than a disaster. The air is crowded to the edges with slow-falling leaves and petals that never reach the ground, tiny skeleton keys tumbling like jewelry constellations, stamped letters and wax-sealed tags orbiting in solemn spirals; corners are packed with miniature dramas—hands reaching from behind shutters that are painted to look like hands, a procession of faceless mannequins carrying blank masks, a mosaic fountain pouring light instead of water. Lighting is hushed and devotional: a cathedral-silence glow, gold leaf radiance diffused through dust, jewel-toned shadows that feel like incense; every surface is ornate, layered, and suspiciously theatrical, as if the town is both birthing the Inventor and consuming him. The wide-lens viewpoint stacks space vertically in dense strata—foreground keys and petals near the viewer, midground balconies and levitating streets, background walls rising like a labyrinthine iconostasis—no empty sky, only patterned architecture and suspended debris; somewhere subtly embedded as a reward (a rug motif, a shop sign, a tile mural half-hidden behind a seal), a small image shows **a mandrill lifting a lion cub**, almost mistaken for decoration, almost mistaken for prophecy.
