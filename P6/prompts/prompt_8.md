A cramped, impossible library-bedroom stitched together with heavy velvet curtains like theater drapes, every surface overcrowded with gilt book spines, tangled bed linens, carved ladders, astrolabes, censers, and map-globes; at the center stands a mythic Idol—an ageless, androgynous figure with the calm authority of a saint and the vulnerability of a stripped confessional—bare skin treated like sacred parchment, posture poised as if mid-coronation yet arrested in time, one knee on a mountain of forbidden tomes, one hand lifted in benediction and warning, their face half-veiled behind a symbolic apparatus of interlocking brass bookstands and a halo-like ring of quill-nibs, so the viewer must peer through machinery and scripture to find them. The taboo is everywhere without being named: a chained book lies open on the bed like a mouth, its pages too white to be innocent, while a key made of bone dangles from the Idol’s throat and a velvet curtain is pinned back with a wedding ring; the room itself reacts as if ashamed—shelves bow inward, curtains blush deeper crimson, flowers grow from margins where no soil exists. The defining impossibility is an Invisible Presence made violently obvious in a frozen instant: an unseen guest sits on the mattress, compressing it; an invisible palm cups the Idol’s cheek leaving a faint dent in skin; a trail of footprints appears in drifting ink across the floorboards; floating teacups and saucers form a spiraling “tea party” around an empty chair, each cup tilted as if being sipped by nothing; quills write by themselves in midair, ink pots uncorking, ink droplets hanging like black pearls, as if the room is being narrated by an absent body. The Eternal Flame of Atar burns physically present in a jeweled brazier built into the headboard—its flame impossibly steady yet casting the shadow of someone who is not there, a second silhouette that does not match the Idol’s anatomy; the flame’s light turns the library into a playful sacrilege, making gilded filigree grin like teeth and turning serious relics into toys. Enforce the Gankutsuou collage illusion: the Idol’s skin, the curtains, the books, and even the shadows are overlaid with flat, unmoving 2D screen-tone textures of clouds and antique maps that do not bend with muscles or folds—static cartography sliding over living forms—so bodies move beneath a stubborn printed veil. Crowd every corner: scattered wildflowers burst from torn encyclopedias and curl around ankles; saucers drift past the foreground like moons; quills and ribbons and bookmarks orbit like insects; cracked inkwells spill upward; tiny mirrors on chains reflect contradictory angles (a hand with no arm, a kiss with no lips), and the entire scene reads as a whimsical, opulent visual riddle paused at the exact instant the unseen trespasser almost touches the forbidden page again.
