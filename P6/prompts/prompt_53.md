Worm’s-eye view from between jagged salt plates and broken sleepers, the camera almost pressed against a towering foreground ornament: a baroque rail-signal shrine fused with living bark, its iron filigree sprouting veins of root that punch upward into impossible architecture—cathedral arches growing directly out of salt dunes, columns that are tree trunks that are station pillars, windows made of petrified leaves, staircases turning into ribcages, nature and masonry braided so tightly the seam is grotesque and unreadable; in the frozen middle of an event, the whole structure is caught mid-bloom/mid-collapse, suspended dust and salt glitter hanging like stopped breath. Centered above, The Elder—mythic, immense, and tired—balances on a skeletal rail line like a tightrope walker at the end of time: stooped yet sovereign posture, one hand cradling a small luminous Tree of Life totem (a branching candelabrum-heart with roots of gold wire) while the other grips a cracked conductor’s cane carved from bone; their face is half-porcelain mask, half-weathered skin, eyes soft with childlike wonder despite the uncanny surroundings; their attire is ceremonial and mismatched—threadbare kimono-coat, velvet epaulettes, antique ticket-seller’s apron—parts slipping to reveal classical, vulnerable nudity treated like sculpture, not erotic, skin etched with map-lines as if the desert wrote on them. Everywhere, the frame is crowded: theater tickets, torn programs, and stamped passes whirl in stopped spirals around the Elder’s ankles like migrating moths; dancing silhouettes (paper-cut figures and shadow-people) cling to rail ties and leap between arches, frozen mid-twirl as if a silent opera is paused; dozens of snowglobes float at different depths, each containing a tiny diorama of the same scene with slight contradictions (the Elder young, the rails underwater, the Tree of Life inverted), reflections multiplying the paradox; miniature stage sets and rail-yard dioramas tumble in the corners, some cracked open to spill mossy marbles and tiny audience seats. The salt desert is not empty: it is densely textured with fossilized tracks, embedded signage, half-buried chandeliers, and root-tunnels that glow from below like a child’s nightlight; skeletal rail lines crisscross and rise into the air as if pulled upward by invisible hands, becoming branch-like scaffolding that cages the sky. Grotesque/uncanny details hide in ornament—faces in the salt crystals, fingernail-like shingles, station clocks that have pupils—yet the lighting is wondrous: jewel-toned auroras and iridescent gold glints turning the horror into a discovery, like finding a forbidden toy theater in a ruin.
