A human-sized abandoned dollhouse mansion yawns open like a hinged ribcage, every room exposed in cross-section at once—nursery, parlor, attic, staircase—crowded with ornate miniatures grown to adult scale: stitched wallpaper seams, button-eyed portraits, moth-eaten velvet drapes, shattered music boxes, drawers spilling diary pages that curl into wings. At the center, The Therapist stands on a broken staircase landing as if on a lectern at the edge of a confession: tall, ageless, serene-faced but exhausted in the eyes, posture forward-leaning in fearless advance, one palm extended in invitation and the other gripping an ankh like a key that could unlock gravity; their attire is ceremonial and intimate at once—half clinical coat, half priestly stole—parts slipping into sensual vulnerability (bare shoulder, exposed collarbone, skin treated like marble under stress), yet never pornographic, the body a classical statue made human by breath. Around them, liberation is literal and paradoxical: the mansion tries to hold everything down, yet the air is full of paused ascent—teacups and saucers hovering mid-spill, stray earrings and hairpins and combs caught in slow cyclone, empty gilded frames floating like windows to nowhere, their interiors showing not scenes but missing memories. The impossible event is mechanical nature erupting through domestic ruin: vines are articulated brass vertebrae with porcelain petals, leaves made of mirror shards reflecting different moments of the therapist’s expression; a flock of gear-driven swallows bursts from an attic cradle, wings of filigree and clock hands, frozen mid-launch as if time itself has been therapized into stillness. Cracks spider across everything—porcelain skin of doll-furniture, mirror-leaf edges, the mansion’s plaster bones—yet the cracks spell unreadable comfort in handwriting. The ankh motif repeats like an obsessive thought: carved into banisters, hanging where chandeliers once were, embedded in the therapist’s throat jewelry like a stethoscope turned relic; one ankh is also the mansion’s central support beam, implying the whole place is balanced on an afterlife symbol. Foreground corners are busy and claustrophobic: a toppled dollhouse throne with mechanical roses growing from its cushions, a tea service orbiting a cracked pocket watch, tiny combs like fallen halo teeth; midground: a corridor of open doors each door a frame with no painting, each frame leaking sand that rises instead of falls; background: an attic sky made of patterned fabric panels, as if the ceiling were a collage of continents.
