A frozen-dream tableau in a lunar tea district carved into crater rims: terraces of stacked teahouses, brass samovars, paper lanterns, and narrow bridges cling to the crater wall like a vertical bazaar, every surface crowded with filigree signage and hanging hook-rails studded with masks—whole porcelain faces, cracked half-smiles, eyeholes rimmed in soot—while loose calendar pages and torn date-squares snow through the air in slow suspension, mingling with drifting feathers that refuse to fall. At the absolute center, a mythic Virgin stands barefoot on a crescent balcony of hammered copper above a steaming tea canal, her posture serene but advancing—chin lifted, shoulders open, one hand pressed to her sternum as if holding a secret light, the other extending forward in fearless invitation; her skin is rendered with classical dignity and vulnerable sensuality, partially veiled by translucent silk that behaves like stained glass rather than fabric. The epiphany is literal: a crystalline beam of “clarity” erupts from her open eyes and splits into duplicated, impossible copies of herself—two, then four, then eight Virgins—each duplicate offset like misregistered prints, all in the same pose yet facing different directions, as if clarity multiplies the self rather than resolves it; the duplicates cast contradictory shadows that point upward into space, and their reflections appear not in water but in the torn calendar pages, where the dates rearrange into unreadable prophecies. Icarus’s wings are physically present as an architectural totem: immense gilded wings bolted into the crater’s rim like a cathedral buttress, feather-plates of patinaed copper and oxidized jade; smaller wing-bones hang like tea-shop signage, and one pair is strapped to the Virgin’s back with ceremonial cords—yet the wings are simultaneously melting and intact, frozen mid-drip, each molten droplet suspended as a perfect lens that refracts tiny duplicated scenes of the district (miniature teahouses, miniature masked patrons) inside it. The impossible event is paused at its most tense moment: a tidal wave of brewed tea is caught mid-crest above the street, holding within it floating hooks, masks, feathers, and calendar pages like fossils in amber; the Virgin walks into this levitating surge as if into a doorway, courageous rather than reckless, while masked onlookers (some masks worn by no bodies, some bodies carrying multiple masks) crowd the stairs and balconies, turning their empty gazes toward her multiplied selves.
