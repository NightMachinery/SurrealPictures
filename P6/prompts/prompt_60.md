A vintage tarot-card plate with crowded, ornate margins and inset mosaics (tiny diptych panels, postage-stamp vignettes, marginalia diagrams) frames a moonlit meadow ringed tight by looming standing stones—no horizon, only layered planes of grass like cut paper, and a sky packed with overlapping constellations, torn map fragments, and faint handwritten letters; at the center stands **The Friend**, mythic and intimate: an androgynous companion with a soft, exhausted smile and tear-wet lashes, half-robed in an antique traveling cloak that slips from one shoulder to reveal classical nude skin like vulnerable marble, their posture protective yet resigned—one hand offering a thread-bare warmth, the other gripping a fractured **broken column** as if it were a cane, a relic, a promise; the meadow itself is a **labyrinth** made of red-thread routes stitched through the grass and across the stones, pinned by countless errant map pins that glitter like dew, each pin anchoring a memory, each thread knotting into impossible circuitry around the Friend’s feet; the “Frozen Dream” catastrophe is blatant: a violent **portal displacement** has snapped the world out of alignment—an oval doorway floats beside the Friend like a ripped-out chapter, and through it the same meadow is visible but rotated and displaced, so standing stones protrude from the portal at wrong angles into this reality, while the Friend’s shadow steps through the portal without their body, and a second, empty cloak collar hangs in midair as if the person has been partially translated; everything is paused mid-event—playing cards hang suspended like caught confetti, tiny bells and ankle-chimes hover with their sound implied but silent, and a rain of map pins drifts inches from impact; in the corners and margins: miniature scenes show the Friend as a child threading needles of starlight, a hand sketching mazes over old love letters, a broken column reassembling then cracking again; the standing stones are carved with half-erased names and labyrinth glyphs, and behind them the moon glows like a tarnished coin—warm, melancholy, nostalgic—casting amber-blue rim light that feels like remembering someone you can’t keep; crucially, all surfaces wear the Gankutsuou collage illusion: **carnival-glass iridescence and confetti screen-tone patterns lie perfectly flat across bodies, stones, cards, and grass—static 2D overlays that do not bend with folds or perspective—so the Friend seems to move beneath a rigid decorative skin**, intensifying the paradox; fill every gap with layered artifacts—thread spools, bell charms, card corners, pinheads, fractured column dust—until the image becomes a crowded riddle asking: which version of the Friend is real, and which is only the route drawn to reach them.
