A frozen-dream tableau inside a funhouse cathedral: warped mirrors fracture the space into a split-level world—an upper nave and a lower crypt that perfectly mirror each other yet disagree in details, like an unreliable recollection; in the center stands a mythic musician (ageless, androgynous, classical anatomy with tasteful partial nudity), spine bowed in quiet devotion as if listening for a song that already ended, hands poised mid-performance on a surreal instrument that is also a lighthouse—its lantern-glass becomes the resonant body, its spiral stairway the neck, its beam trapped in a gilded cage like a captive note, throwing warm melancholic light across both levels. Weather is contained and defiant: a thunderstorm is bottled in cathedral reliquaries, rain and hail suspended inside floating perfume flacons and birdcages, lightning folded into neat ribbons behind mirror panes, while a small private sunset swirls inside a hanging jar near the musician’s throat; outside-weather tries to leak in as snowflakes caught in sewing thread, but everything is paused mid-fall, mid-splash, mid-gust, motion implied yet perfectly still. The entire scene is crowded—foreground needles pinned into velvet pews and choir rails like constellations, thread spools unraveling into labyrinthine lines that stitch reflections to reality; origami boats drift through the air as if sailing on invisible floodwater, some caught on mirror edges, some perched in the musician’s hair, some sinking into tar-black puddles that reflect childhood rooms; dancing silhouettes (not fully human, cutout-sharp) waltz between gilded cages and organ pipes, their shadows performing a different choreography than their bodies, hinting at forgotten partners. Nostalgia permeates the architecture: sheet music becomes faded letters, photographs, ticket stubs, and pressed feathers, all embedded in mirror frames; the lower world shows the same objects but aged, water-stained, or reversed, as if memory edited them.
