You are creating a single illustration for a collectible Dixit card designed for adults --- a surrealist artwork that functions as a visual Rorschach test. The image must be polysemic: five different people should look at it and project five completely different stories, emotions, and memories onto it. It should never illustrate a single readable narrative. Instead, it should feel like a half-remembered dream the viewer is convinced they have had before but cannot quite place.

The primary artistic tradition is Gustav Klimt's gilded Art Nouveau world --- iridescent gold leaf, jewel-tone palettes of sapphire, ruby, emerald, and amethyst, organic spiraling flourishes, and a devotion to decorative saturation that borders on the sacred. Recall how Klimt's figures emerge from golden pattern-fields in states of undress, their nakedness inseparable from the ornamentation surrounding them. That fusion is your model: the human body treated the way Klimt, Schiele, Mucha, and the great classical painters treated it --- as a site of vulnerability, desire, power, and unresolved contradiction, never as mere decoration. A nude or partially nude figure entangled with architecture, consumed by pattern, dissolving into landscape, or simply existing in raw unguarded stillness within a fever-dream setting --- these collisions between flesh and fantasy are where the most potent ambiguity lives. Do not clothe a figure out of caution when nakedness would make the image more haunting, more tender, more unsettling, or more beautiful. The figure should appear caught in a private, unperformed moment --- something the viewer feels they are intruding upon, something that makes them uncertain whether they are witnessing ecstasy or anguish, liberation or surrender.

Built on this Klimt foundation is a specific rendering technique applied as a global rule. Every visible surface without exception --- skin, hair, clothing if any, architecture, sky, water, stone --- must be filled with a rich intricate pattern texture: brocade, damask, circuitry, thorn filigree, stained glass, woodgrain, starfields, lacework. On bare skin this effect is especially striking: the nude body becomes a living tapestry, flesh and ornament fused into something hovering in an uncanny space between icon and organism. Crucially, these pattern layers must remain perfectly flat and static, like a collage cutout or a window into a separate decorative dimension. They never warp, bend, stretch, or follow the three-dimensional curvature of the forms they cover. The 3D shapes underneath may twist, fold, and recede freely, but the pattern skins stay locked and unmoving --- a hypnotic visual paradox between flat ornament and volumetric form. This is not a selective flourish; it is the fundamental visual logic of the entire image.

The emotional register is adult in every dimension --- capable of evoking longing, shame, ecstasy, grief, quiet dread, or tenderness depending on who is looking and what they bring to it. Every object should feel symbolic but refuse to resolve into a single meaning. A key could be an escape, a secret, a loss, or a promise. A wilting flower could be grief, relief, surrender, or grace. Stack these ambiguities densely so the image becomes a cabinet of interpretive possibilities.

The composition must be maximalist and claustrophobic in its richness. Every square inch --- foreground, midground, background, corners, margins --- should be occupied by meaningful visual information. No empty sky, no bare walls, no negative space for breathing room. Light should feel both cosmic and intimate, as if the scene is lit by something alive and watching.

The mood is a frozen dream: one impossible instant suspended at the precise tipping point between two irreconcilable states --- beauty and ruin, intimacy and vastness, tenderness and threat, the sacred and the grotesque. Time has stopped not peacefully but urgently, as if the scene is holding its breath. The longer the viewer looks, the more they should discover, and the less certain they should become about what they are actually seeing or feeling.
