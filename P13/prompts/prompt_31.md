A rain-soaked bus stop built inside a vast abandoned textile factory where towering looms rise like gothic cathedral arches, their warp threads stretching into the rafters like constellations; a small neighborhood has gathered around a long feast table set directly beneath the machines—bowls of steaming soup, stitched tablecloths, candles in thimbles—quietly performing a “rain ritual” by catching falling drops in glass jars and hanging them on the loom threads as if storing weather. At the table’s edge, a gentle, shaggy beast sits like an honored guest, letting a child braid its fur with ribbon, while a melancholic elder smiles through grief. In the foreground, a single lost slipper lies on a puddle’s surface like a buoyant relic; its heel has become an ornate key, and inside the puddle a pale spirit reflection reaches upward toward it, as if the water is a doorway. Behind a support pillar, a masked ninja figure half-hidden in shadow offers a caduceus made of twined yarn and silver needles, the serpents subtly alive—one thread-serpent glowing warm, the other cool—suggesting healing, secrecy, and invitation. Background packed with story details: spools like stained-glass windows, hanging garments that resemble sleeping bodies, weather maps pinned to fabric bolts, community hands mending torn banners, distant loom belts moving without operators.
