A monsoon harbor crowded with towering lacquered pagoda ships, rain slanting like silver threads; on the slick deck of one ship stands a freestanding doorframe with no walls, its threshold glowing warmly as if it opens into a quiet interior. A parent and child huddle in intimate closeness beside the doorway, sheltering under a shipboard “maypole” made from a mast wrapped in ribboning ropes and prayer-streamers that whip in the wind; the ribbons trail down into the floodwater below—but the water has turned to *solid liquidity*, holding the ribbons in slow frozen arcs like glass caught mid-splash. From the solid-water surface rises an enormous open hand, palm up, offering a small ceramic vessel that is somehow also a tiny boat; inside it, a white guitar rests like an heirloom, strings faintly humming with the storm. A nearby sailor wears a red war-paint mask, half-lifted as if about to reveal a familiar face, watching the doorway with hesitant tenderness. Background packed with story: stacked lanterns reflecting in the glassy flood, distant cranes, hanging charms, wet teak textures, miniature markets on neighboring decks, drifting paper boats, thunderclouds shaped like curled waves.
