A flooded night market under endless arched arcades that recede in repeated vanishing points like a corridor into infinity; narrow gondolas glide between submerged stalls piled with floating spices, paper lanterns, cracked mirrors, and tangled seaweed bouquets. In the foreground, a tired ferrymaker wearing a small grape-soda bottle-cap badge performs a quiet ritual: lowering a brass bucket into a mossy wishing well that inexplicably stands on a stone island amid the water; as the bucket rises, the well projects shimmering “memories” onto the air—ghostly scenes playing across hanging fabrics and puddle surfaces like living frescoes. Beside the well, a black cat sits in a delicate cage of reeds and filigree, eyes bright and accusing, while a death’s-head moth the size of a hand clings to the cage door, its wings patterned like a warning mask. Behind them looms the Guardian: a weathered statue with a cracked halo, half-swallowed by vines and coral, one hand raised as if to halt the tide. Above, a phoenix made of sparks and petals erupts from the projected memories, shedding explosive vitality—new flowers and tiny birds bursting into existence midair—yet the bloom feels slightly wrong, too fast, too loud for the quiet scene. The entire composition echoes in kaleidoscopic quadrant repetition across the market (symmetrical reflections in water, duplicated lantern strings, mirrored stalls), but one intentional break disrupts the pattern: a single quadrant shows dawn light instead of night, and the ferrymaker’s shadow points the opposite direction.
