A solitary hermit stands perfectly still on a cliff at the edge of a cinder valley, his cloak a shimmering golden fleece that catches the wind like a flag of quiet victory; one gloved palm is held open toward the viewer as if offering a choice, while a black cat with a crooked tail coils calmly around his boots, watching. From the hermit’s chest—where emotion seems tightly sealed—delicate ice architecture bursts outward in slow, crystalline eruptions: frozen staircases, cathedral spires, and balconies growing like coral, fusing with living roots and ivy that thread through the ice and turn it into impossible habitable ruins. Above his open hand hovers a death’s-head moth the size of a lantern, wings beating so softly they stir snow-dust from the ice; its patterns resemble a smiling skull mask, and each wingbeat releases visible “notes” of color—saffron, indigo, and peppermint-green—that drift through the air like scented music (sensory synesthesia). The foreground remains hushed and museum-still—cat, hermit, open hand, frost—while the background is kinetic chaos: smoking glass dunes ripple like waves, embers skitter across the sand, distant arches collapse and regrow, and flocks of paper-thin birds spiral through ash-laced wind. In the valley, half-buried doorways made of bone-white ice open onto warm, impossible interiors, hinting at stories inside; faint silhouettes celebrate behind translucent walls as if triumph is happening somewhere just out of reach.
