Loose letters shake free and become small paper birds, spiraling over the water and pecking at the canal’s rippling surface, which briefly turns to ink. Onlookers freeze mid-step with half-finished gestures, lantern light caught in their widened eyes, while the bridge’s shadow stretches the wrong direction and the first stair treads already erase the mossy cobbles beneath them.
Vertical 2:3 composition: In a narrow canal-street at dusk, a lone cobbler stands on a wobbling ladder, mid-reach, pulling a bright copper shoelace straight out of his own chest like a stubborn thread, trying to lace shut a widening tear in the cobblestones; as he yanks, the street buckles and folds upward like fabric, and tiny doorways tumble open along the new creases, spilling miniature rooms into the open air. His face is calm but strained, as if pretending the impossible is routine, while bystanders—faceless mannequins in raincoats—hold their breath, fingers hovering near the gap without touching. In the background, a silent carousel drifts half-submerged in the canal, its horses replaced by empty coats that sway as if inhabited. Soft amber streetlamps glow through fog, casting long, uneasy shadows that point the wrong way.
A cartographer stands on a wobbling stool in a cramped attic, mid-swipe with a pale eraser across a sprawling wall map whose ink lines shimmer like wet thread; as the coastline vanishes under their hand, the erased space immediately becomes real—cool white fog pours out of the paper and spills into the room, curling around trunks, teacups, and stacks of yellowed letters. The cartographer’s other hand tries to pin the map down, but the parchment buckles and breathes, tugging toward the open skylight where dusk presses in. Tiny compass roses detach from the map and drift like moths, landing on the cartographer’s sleeves, marking them with new directions they didn’t choose. With every second, the fog thickens, lifting loose objects a finger’s width off the floor, as if the missing place is demanding to be entered now.
