Center foreground: the Dreamer—mythic, ageless, and ambiguously gendered—kneels on a velvet stair that shouldn’t exist, torso bare beneath an inside‑out silk coat whose seams and labels trail like soft tongues; their expression is half-hidden by a cracked mirror-mask, but their hands are unmistakably gentle as they cradle the Beloved against their chest the way one would hold an injured bird. The Beloved is both person and artifact (skin like warm marble, hair like spilled ink in water), eyes closed in trust; their posture suggests surrender, sleep, or aftermath—unclear which—and the Dreamer’s fingertips are stitching something back together: not flesh, but reality itself. The Eternal Flame of Atar burns without consuming: a small, unwavering white-gold fire cupped between the Dreamer and the Beloved like a shared breath, reflected infinitely in scattered shards of glass; it lights bruises into blossoms, turns tears into beads, and makes ash behave like snow.
