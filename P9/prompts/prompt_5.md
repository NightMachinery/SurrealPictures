At the center, a mythic Shah-turned-Traveler stands at the threshold of a miniature “home” built from interlocking cogs and folding walls—half throne room, half suitcase—wearing a travel-worn royal cloak over bare, vulnerable skin (tasteful classical nudity suggested by slipping silk and ornate jewelry), crown tilted like a compass needle, one hand gripping a lantern pole, the other holding a sealed letter and a heavy ring of keys; their posture is both command and exile, as if blessing and abandoning the same shelter. Gravity is unmistakably reversed: molten droplets, tools, petals, and loose pages fall upward toward the ceiling; boots and chairs hover mid-tumble; a second “ground” exists above where upside-down figures stroll calmly, their shadows drifting the wrong way; conveyor belts climb vertically and loop into the rafters like suspended rivers. A lantern festival erupts through the foundry: dozens of paper lanterns (some heartlike, some coffinlike, some shaped like rooms) drift in both directions, snagging on gears, illuminating hidden alcoves; a few lanterns are tethered to the Shah’s cloak as if the light itself is trying to come home. Cutting through the industrial interior is a luminous rainbow bridge—an impossible Bifrost made from refracted furnace light and dyed silk belts—arching from the tiny central house-threshold to the inverted ceiling-walkway, with colors pooling like spilled candy on metal and turning soot into confetti. The heraldic lion appears everywhere at once in contradictory forms: a proud lion rampant embossed on a banner, a mechanical lion-head casting mold breathing frozen sparks, and a living lion made of stitched velvet and riveted brass sitting like a guardian at the doorway—its mane a halo of watch-springs—watching the Shah/Traveler with tenderness that could also be judgment.
